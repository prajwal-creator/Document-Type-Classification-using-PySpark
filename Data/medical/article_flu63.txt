Mr. Walker, who made his name in the San Francisco Bay Area, demonstrated one capability after another in his first set on Tuesday night at the Blue Note, 131 West Third Street in Greenwich Village, where he is appearing through  tomorrow night with Ike Turner sitting in. His songs sketched classic blues situations, mostly in generic terms, with carefully varied rhythms: slow-grind, rockabilly, funk, Chicago blues shuffles. But they were simply vehicles for his musical skills. His gutsy tenor voice could rise smoothly, push toward a fervent rasp, sweep downward to confess trouble or leap to exultant falsetto whoops. But his priority was guitar solos: clear, wiry phrases with a new strategy for each chorus. He played light, skipping triplets and wailing B. B. King-sustained notes; he played  stinging lines and skittering bursts of speed; he played solos that jumped around the guitar and solos that latched onto one phrase again and again. He also played harmonica, with high, single-note lines suggesting patience about to break.   Advertisement   Continue reading the main story   It took Mr. Turner's arrival to show what was missing. Mr. Turner, before his years with Tina Turner, was one of the band leaders who pushed rhythm-and-blues toward rock-and-roll, and he still brings the music a sense of impulsiveness and risk. He didn't share any of Mr. Walker's songs about woman trouble, perhaps so as not to remind listeners of the accusations Ms. Turner made against him in her autobiography.
